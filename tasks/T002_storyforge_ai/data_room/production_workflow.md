# Production Workflow

StoryForge compared its current AI-assisted workflow with a traditional outsourced comic drama workflow used in its first pilot projects.

## Before AI-Assisted Workflow

Typical workflow:

1. Human writer drafts 8-episode arc.
2. External illustrator creates character sheets and panels.
3. Motion editor adapts panels for short-form video.
4. Internal team reviews continuity and pacing.
5. Distribution team cuts platform-specific hooks.

Average staffing per active series:

- 1.0 writer
- 1.5 illustrators
- 0.5 motion editor
- 0.5 producer/editor

Management estimates that the old workflow required 14 calendar days and $6,800 per finished episode.

## AI-Assisted Workflow

Current workflow:

1. Human writer drafts character bible and episode outline.
2. AI tools generate concept art, backgrounds, and first-pass panels.
3. Internal editor selects, revises, and re-prompts assets.
4. Motion template system creates short-form cuts.
5. Human QA checks continuity, content safety, and platform fit.

Average staffing per active series:

- 0.5 writer
- 0.3 visual editor
- 0.2 motion/template editor
- 0.3 producer/editor

Management estimates that the current workflow requires 4.5 calendar days and $2,450 per finished episode.

## Operational Caveats

- The AI workflow performs best in romance, fantasy revenge, and workplace comedy genres.
- Continuity errors increase when a series runs beyond 20 episodes.
- Human editor workload spikes for character consistency and sensitive scenes.
- Current tooling does not automatically verify asset provenance.
- The workflow is faster for visual production, but story quality still depends heavily on human editors.
